# **CHAPTER 18 LECTURE ROADMAP: China in Decline (1800–1900)**

## **Series Overview**

**Series Title:** "The Century of Humiliation: Crisis, Response, and the Question of Chinese Civilization"

**Time Period:** 1800–1900

**Series Learning Objectives:**
1. Analyze the interplay between internal structural problems and Western imperialism in causing Qing decline
2. Evaluate different Chinese responses to crisis—from millenarian rebellion to self-strengthening to radical reform—and explain why each failed or succeeded
3. Compare China's trajectory with Meiji Japan to understand different outcomes of the "Western challenge"
4. Assess the role of Empress Dowager Cixi as both political operator and obstacle to reform

**Connection Between Lectures:**
- **Lecture 1** establishes the structural crisis (internal) and the Western "shock" (external), keeping these in tension
- **Lecture 2** examines the most dramatic internal response—the Taiping Rebellion—as both symptom of crisis and catalyst for change
- **Lecture 3** traces elite responses (Self-Strengthening, Hundred Days Reform) and asks the comparative question: why did China's reforms fail where Japan's succeeded?

**How This Complements the Textbook:**
- Goes deeper on causation and historiographical debates (internal vs. external factors)
- Develops Cixi as a complex figure across all three lectures rather than a sidebar
- Provides sustained comparative analysis with Japan that the textbook only sketches
- Students watch a video on the Boxer Rebellion, so Lecture 3 can reference it without covering it in depth

## **LECTURE 1: "Cracks in the Empire" — The Opium War and the Anatomy of Decline**

**Learning Objectives:**
- Identify the internal structural problems facing the Qing by 1800 (population pressure, fiscal crisis, military decay, Manchu identity tensions)
- Explain how the opium trade created a crisis and why the Qing response led to war
- Analyze the Treaty of Nanjing and the "unequal treaty system" as the beginning of China's "Century of Humiliation"

**Lecture Theme:** The Opium War was not a bolt from the blue—it struck an empire already weakened from within. This lecture holds internal and external factors in tension, showing how they reinforced each other.

**Central Story:** Lin Zexu's moral crusade against opium—a righteous official who did everything right and still lost. His story dramatizes the gap between Qing expectations and new global realities.

**Cixi Thread (Setup):** Brief introduction—in 1839, when Lin Zexu is destroying opium, the future Empress Dowager is a four-year-old girl in a declining bannerman family. Her world is about to be transformed.

**Structure Outline:**

**Opening Hook (5–7 min):**
- Display image: "Destruction of Opium at Humen" (1839)
- Question: "What does it take to destroy 2.6 million pounds of opium? And what happens when you do?"
- Preview the paradox: Lin Zexu was competent, moral, and strategic—and he triggered a catastrophe

**Core Content Segment 1: "The Empire at 1800" (12–15 min)**
- Population explosion: 300 million to 400 million in 50 years
- Fiscal crisis: revenues flat, expenses rising, infrastructure decaying (Grand Canal example)
- Military decay: banner armies as welfare system, Green Standard armies ineffective
- Manchu identity problem: defenders of privilege or agents of reform?
- **Pause & Process #1:** "Think about a large organization you know—a company, a government, a school. What happens when it grows faster than its management systems can handle?"

**Core Content Segment 2: "The Opium Crisis" (12–15 min)**
- British trade problem: silver flowing out, need for return cargo
- Opium as solution: addiction economics
- Qing debates: legalize and tax vs. suppress utterly
- Lin Zexu's appointment and strategy
- The destruction at Humen and British response
- Why gunboats beat junks: technological and organizational gap

**Core Content Segment 3: "The Unequal Treaties" (10–12 min)**
- Treaty of Nanjing (1842): indemnity, Hong Kong, five ports, no more Canton system
- Treaty of the Bogue: extraterritoriality, most-favored-nation
- Why these terms mattered: sovereignty shredded
- Chinese elite response: outrage and denial
- Lin Zexu's fate: exile, then rehabilitation—but the world had changed
- **Pause & Process #2:** "The Qing saw themselves as the center of civilization. The British saw themselves as bringing 'free trade' and 'progress.' How do these worldviews make negotiation almost impossible?"

**Closing (5 min):**
- The Opium War exposed the rot—but didn't cause it
- Internal and external factors now feeding each other
- Preview: "Next time, we'll see what happens when a failed examination candidate decides he's the brother of Jesus Christ—and nearly topples the dynasty."

**Key Terms:** Cohong system, Lin Zexu, Treaty of Nanjing, extraterritoriality, most-favored-nation clause, "unequal treaties"

## **LECTURE 2: "The Heavenly Kingdom" — The Taiping Rebellion and the Transformation of China**

**Learning Objectives:**
- Explain the origins of the Taiping movement in the context of social crisis and religious syncretism
- Analyze the Taiping program (land reform, gender equality, anti-Manchu nationalism) and why it attracted millions
- Evaluate the Qing response and the rise of regional armies under figures like Zeng Guofan

**Lecture Theme:** The Taiping Rebellion was the deadliest civil war in human history—and it reveals everything about what was broken in mid-century China and what it would take to survive.

**Central Story:** Hong Xiuquan's journey from failed examination candidate to self-proclaimed brother of Jesus Christ to ruler of a theocratic state—a story of religious vision, political radicalism, and catastrophic violence.

**Cixi Thread (Development):** In 1861, as the Taiping war grinds on, Cixi engineers a palace coup to become regent for her infant son. She inherits a dynasty fighting for survival—and the generals who save it will become power brokers she must manage.

**Historiographical Context (10 min):** The Taiping have been interpreted as proto-communists (Mao's view), Christian martyrs, peasant revolutionaries, or dangerous fanatics. How we frame them depends on what we think "progress" means.

**Structure Outline:**

**Opening Hook (5–7 min):**
- Display: Map showing Taiping territory at maximum extent (1853–1856)—huge swath of central China
- Statistic: "Between 20 and 30 million dead. More than World War I. What could cause this?"
- Introduce Hong Xiuquan: a man who failed the examination four times and then had visions

**Core Content Segment 1: "A Prophet Rises" (12–15 min)**
- Hong's background: Hakka outsider, examination failure, psychological crisis
- The visions and the Christian tracts: a syncretic theology is born
- God Worshippers Society: why it appealed (anti-opium, anti-gambling, community, hope)
- The march north and the establishment of the Heavenly Kingdom at Nanjing (1853)
- **Pause & Process #1:** "Hong offered certainty in a world of chaos—a clear explanation of what was wrong and a promise of a new order. Think of other movements (religious or political) that have offered similar appeals in times of crisis."

**Core Content Segment 2: "The Heavenly Kingdom's Program" (10–12 min)**
- Land reform: equal distribution by household
- Gender revolution: women soldiers, foot-binding banned, separate men's and women's armies
- Anti-Manchu nationalism: "drive out the demons," queue-cutting
- The contradictions: Hong's harem, internal purges, theological incoherence
- Why it attracted millions—and why it alienated the elite

**Core Content Segment 3: "How the Qing Survived" (12–15 min)**
- Regular armies fail repeatedly
- Zeng Guofan and the Xiang Army: a new model (Confucian officers, local recruitment, regional funding)
- Li Hongzhang and the Huai Army: the next generation
- Slow strangulation of Nanjing (1864): mass suicide, mass slaughter
- The cost: regional militarization, the court depends on Han generals
- **Pause & Process #2:** "The Qing survived by empowering regional strongmen. What are the long-term risks of that strategy?"

**Closing (5 min):**
- Cixi's position: she owes her throne to men like Zeng and Li
- The generals who saved the dynasty will now try to reform it
- Preview: "Next time—Self-Strengthening, the rise of Japan, and the question that haunts modern China: why couldn't we modernize?"

**Key Terms:** Hong Xiuquan, God Worshippers Society, Taiping Tianguo (Heavenly Kingdom of Great Peace), Zeng Guofan, Xiang Army, Li Hongzhang

- "Zeng Guofan Qing general photograph"
- "Nanjing destruction 1864 Taiping fall"

**Primary Source Option:** Taiping land system proclamation (excerpts) or Hong Xiuquan's religious visions

## **LECTURE 3: "Self-Strengthening and Its Discontents" — Reform, Failure, and the Japan Comparison**

**Learning Objectives:**
- Explain the Self-Strengthening Movement's goals, methods, and limitations
- Analyze why the Hundred Days Reform failed and Cixi's role in its suppression
- Compare Chinese and Japanese responses to Western pressure and evaluate explanations for different outcomes

**Lecture Theme:** China tried to modernize—arsenals, shipyards, schools, even constitutional reform. Why did it fail where Japan succeeded? The answer reveals deep questions about institutions, culture, and political will.

**Central Story:** The arc from Feng Guifen's essays (1861) to Kang Youwei's Hundred Days (1898) to Cixi's coup—a forty-year struggle over how much China must change to survive.

**Cixi Thread (Culmination):** Cixi emerges as the central figure—a woman who clawed her way to power in a system designed to exclude her, who supported Self-Strengthening when it suited her and crushed radical reform when it threatened her. Was she a villain or a realist?

**Historiographical Context (10 min):** The "why not China?" question—modernization theory, world-systems theory, institutional explanations, cultural explanations. Each framework yields different answers and different politics.

**Structure Outline:**

**Opening Hook (5–7 min):**
- Display: Photo of Li Hongzhang and photo of Itō Hirobumi (Japanese PM) side by side
- "Both of these men tried to modernize their countries. One succeeded. One failed. Why?"
- This is the question that haunted Chinese intellectuals for a century

**Core Content Segment 1: "Self-Strengthening: The First Attempt" (12–15 min)**
- Feng Guifen's formula: "Chinese learning for the essence, Western learning for practical use"
- What they built: Jiangnan Arsenal, Fuzhou Shipyard, translation bureaus, foreign language schools
- Li Hongzhang's projects: timeline of initiatives (1862–1893)
- The limits: provincial, piecemeal, underfunded, opposed by conservatives
- The test: Sino-French War (1884–1885)—mixed results
- **Pause & Process #1:** "Self-Strengthening tried to adopt Western technology without Western institutions. Can you separate technology from the social systems that produce it?"

**Core Content Segment 2: "Cixi, the Reformers, and the Hundred Days" (12–15 min)**
- Sino-Japanese War (1894–1895): the shock of losing to Japan
- Kang Youwei and Liang Qichao: radical reform from Confucian premises
- The young Guangxu emperor's gamble: 103 reform edicts in 103 days
- What they proposed: constitutional monarchy, new schools, abolish examinations, restructure government
- Why conservatives panicked: bannermen, examination degree-holders, Cixi's own position
- Cixi's coup: Guangxu under house arrest, reformers executed or fled
- Was Cixi defending Manchu privilege or preventing chaos?
- **Pause & Process #2:** "Cixi is often blamed for China's failure to reform. But she supported Self-Strengthening for decades. What changed in 1898? What was she actually protecting?"

**Core Content Segment 3: "The Japan Comparison" (10–12 min)**
- What Japan did differently: Meiji Restoration as revolution from above
- Institutional transformation: abolished feudalism, created conscript army, wrote constitution
- Political will: oligarchs agreed on direction, could override opposition
- China's obstacles: Manchu rulers couldn't abolish their own privileges; regionalism; no unified elite
- The comparative question: Was it leadership? Institutions? Timing? Geography? Size?
- Avoiding determinism: choices mattered, nothing was inevitable

**Closing (5–8 min):**
- Reference the Boxer Rebellion video: "You've seen what happens next—popular rage, foreign intervention, final humiliation"
- By 1900: China indebted, occupied, seemingly helpless
- But the story doesn't end there—the next chapter covers how a new generation will conclude that the dynasty itself must go
- Final question: "Was the Qing's failure inevitable? Or could different choices have produced a different outcome?"

**Key Terms:** Self-Strengthening Movement, "Chinese essence, Western use" (zhongti xiyong), Feng Guifen, Sino-Japanese War (1894–1895), Kang Youwei, Liang Qichao, Hundred Days Reform, Meiji Restoration
