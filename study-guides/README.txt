# HIST 270 — Chapter 1 (Bronze Age China) Project README

## Purpose

This README documents the development of **Study Guide 2.0** for HIST 270, Chapter 1 (Bronze Age China). The purpose of this work is to **update older study guides** so they align more effectively with:

* the textbook chapter (**East Asia: A Cultural, Social, and Political History, 3rd ed.**) and
* the redesigned **Reveal.js lecture decks** used in the course.

In other words: the goal is not merely to modernize formatting, but to **tighten conceptual alignment** so the study guide cues students toward what the chapter and lectures emphasize as most important.

## Core Pedagogical Innovation: The 4 Big Takeaways

The Study Guide 2.0 begins with **4 Big Takeaways**, each requiring students to produce:

* **exactly 3 bullet points**
* **5–10 words per bullet**
* no paragraphs

### Big Takeaway #1 — Evidence

**“How do we know the Shang were real?”**

Targets:

* oracle bones as inscriptions
* verification via evidence
* transition from myth to historical confidence

### Big Takeaway #2 — Power

**“How did Shang kings rule?”**

Targets:

* ritual power + military power
* divination + sacrifice as governance tools
* king as priest/general

### Big Takeaway #3 — Legitimacy

**“Why did Zhou invent the Mandate of Heaven?”**

Targets:

* conquest legitimacy problem
* Mandate = conditional, moral political theory
* overthrow becomes explainable/legitimate

### Big Takeaway #4 — Diversity

**“Why does Sanxingdui matter?”**

Targets:

* regional cultural plurality in Bronze Age China
* critique of “Yellow River = all China”
* Sanxingdui as coeval and sophisticated culture

## Key Upgrade Added Later

### Evidence Method Insert

We added a specific “method” question to ensure students absorb the lecture’s historical-method logic:

> The Shang are “historically verified” because historians combine ______ evidence with ______ evidence.

Answer key:

* archaeological evidence + written/inscriptional evidence

This was added to better reflect the methodological framing of Lecture 1.

---

## Built-In Misconception Prevention

### Common Mistakes to Avoid (Section)

We added a “Common Mistakes to Avoid” section to prevent predictable student errors, especially:

* Shang ≠ Mandate of Heaven
* Mandate ≠ divine right forever
* Zhou ≠ medieval European feudalism (not identical)
* Sanxingdui ≠ later than Shang (coeval overlap)
* oracle bones are primary evidence, not myth
* “China” not naturally unified from beginning

This section is designed as a high-impact, low-effort intervention before quizzes.
